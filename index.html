Single Line Drawings

Architects and designers work with geometry, but they do not build geometry. Geometry is abstract and cannot be realized in physical form. Even with the most precise digital tools, it is impossible to build a circle. What we are left with is a shape defined by the translation of geometric properties into material processes. These processes are embodied in the light transmitted to a LED screen, ink deposited on a paper or in the material extruded from the nozzle on a 3D printer. In most cases, architects make decisions about geometry to produce shapes which are then translated into material either by humans or computational processes. A contractor may produce a set of shop drawings to reconcile the forms and shapes present in an architectural drawing with the material processes of construction. Most 3d printers contain slicing software that translates mesh geometries into tool paths and extrusion rates. In both cases, the architectâ€™s work is distinct from the material processes. Another option, and one being increasingly explored in Robotic fabrication, is to directly link the design of an object to its material processes of construction. Computational tools are used to develop custom tool paths, mechanisms, sensors and workspaces that allow the designer to design not only objects but processes for the materialization of form. This semester we will explore the connection between computational design and material processes through a set of projects that examine the process of 3D printing.
